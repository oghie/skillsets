# Integrity And Source Audit

Use this file when auditing references, citations, research ethics, statistical integrity, or publication-readiness risks.

## Anti-Hallucination Rules

- Do not invent citations, DOI links, page numbers, quotations, journal metrics, acceptance rates, indexing, datasets, statistical results, or source claims.
- Do not silently correct a citation if the source cannot be verified.
- Do not treat a plausible title or DOI pattern as evidence.
- If metadata, ranking, guidelines, or citation counts matter, verify them live when tools are available.
- If live verification is unavailable, state exactly what still needs verification.

## Source Audit

For each important source, check:
- Does the source exist?
- Do author, year, title, outlet, volume/issue/pages, DOI/URL, and publisher match?
- Does the cited source support the sentence it is attached to?
- Is the source primary evidence, review evidence, commentary, policy guidance, or background?
- Is the source current enough for the claim?
- Is contradictory evidence missing?
- Is the citation style internally consistent?

## Research Ethics

For human-participant or sensitive data work, check:
- Ethics review or exemption.
- Consent or justified waiver.
- Privacy and de-identification.
- Participant safety and distress protocols.
- Special protection for vulnerable participants.
- Conflict of interest and funding disclosure.
- Data access, storage, and sharing constraints.
- Responsible use of administrative, platform, scraped, or sensitive data.

Ethics absence is a serious risk when the study includes participants, identifiable records, intervention, deception, sensitive topics, or vulnerable groups.

## Statistical Integrity

Treat significance tests as narrow tools for reasoning about sampling error under assumptions.

Check:
- Statistically significant results also have magnitude, uncertainty, and practical meaning.
- Large samples are not used to inflate trivial effects.
- Nonrandom samples make formal significance claims more tentative.
- Multiple testing, model shopping, outlier removal, missing-data handling, and imputation choices are transparent.
- Hypotheses were specified before analysis when confirmatory claims are made.
- Exploratory analyses are labeled exploratory.
- Robustness and sensitivity checks address plausible alternatives.
- Replication or external validation is discussed for surprising claims.

Red flags:
- "Significant" used as a synonym for important.
- P-value thresholds treated as the whole argument.
- Unsupported deletion of outliers.
- Many models with only favorable results reported.
- Causal language from cross-sectional association.
- No descriptive statistics for key variables.

## Publication And Journal Claims

Current journal facts need live verification:
- Scope and aims.
- Author guidelines.
- Word limits.
- Article types.
- Reporting standards.
- Open access fees.
- Indexing and rankings.
- Impact factor and quartile.
- Acceptance rate and review timeline.

Do not recommend submission to a specific journal as ready without checking fit, scope, article type, manuscript length, reference style, and required declarations.
