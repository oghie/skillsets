# Methods Evaluation Matrix

Use this file when judging whether methods support the intended inference.

## Sampling And Generalization

Check:
- Target population or case universe.
- Sampling frame and recruitment pathway.
- Probability sampling when broad population generalization is claimed.
- Stratification, clustering, weighting, or oversampling when subgroup claims matter.
- Response rate, nonresponse bias, and attempts to improve participation.
- Convenience or opt-in sample limitations.
- Total-population data limits when generalizing beyond the observed population, place, or time.
- Sample size and subgroup size relative to design and analysis.
- Demographic, contextual, or site detail needed for transferability.

## Context-Specific And Non-Generalizable Research

Do not force broad-generalization criteria onto studies whose purpose is case insight, mechanism discovery, feasibility, participation, or thick contextual understanding.

Check:
- Whether generalization is intended.
- Whether participants, sites, documents, or cases are selected for analytic fit.
- Ethnography: access, immersion, insider knowledge, culture/group alignment.
- Phenomenology: participants have direct lived experience of the phenomenon.
- Action research: relevant stakeholders participate meaningfully in design, action, reflection, and revision.
- Case study: case-selection rationale and boundary conditions.
- Pilot study: sample is relevant for testing feasibility, procedures, measures, and risks.
- Participant identity protection is credible, especially with small samples or sensitive settings.

## Measurement And Data Quality

Check:
- Conceptual definition -> operational definition -> data source alignment.
- Item wording, response format, instrument source, restrictions, and examples.
- Sensitive topics: privacy, anonymity, rapport, question order, mode, underreporting, social desirability, safety.
- Observations: training, protocol, reactivity, unobtrusiveness, inter-observer reliability.
- Secondary/big data: provenance, missingness, selection bias, construct drift, linkage error, platform or administrative incentives.
- Reliability: internal consistency for scales, temporal stability for stable traits, interrater/intercoder agreement for subjective coding.
- Validity: content coverage, face plausibility, empirical association with relevant criteria, construct alignment, descriptive accuracy, interpretive fit, theoretical fit.
- Triangulation when one measure is likely biased or incomplete.

## Experiments And Quasi-Experiments

Check:
- Random assignment is distinguished from random sampling.
- Treatment and control groups are comparable.
- Participants experience conditions similarly except for the treatment.
- Demand characteristics, placebo effects, expectancy effects, and Hawthorne effects are considered.
- Group sizes are adequate.
- Attrition is reported and analyzed.
- Outcome assessors are blind when subjective judgment is involved.
- Outcomes are objective or measured with credible instruments.
- Quasi-experiments address baseline equivalence, selection, history effects, maturation, regression to the mean, and other alternatives.
- Treatment details, administrator training, quality assurance, compliance, contamination, and fidelity are reported.
- Natural-setting trade-offs and ethical acceptability are addressed.

## Quantitative Analysis

Check:
- Descriptive statistics are available for key variables.
- Percentages are paired with counts.
- Means are not used alone for skewed distributions.
- Inferential tests match data type, design, sampling, and assumptions.
- Statistical significance is not treated as effect size.
- Substantive magnitude and practical significance are interpreted.
- Nonrandom samples make significance claims more tentative.
- Model choices, controls, missing data, outliers, and robustness are transparent.
- Tables and figures make the results easier to understand.
- Results answer the research question rather than displaying every model run.

## Qualitative Methods And Analysis

Check:
- Specific design or tradition is named when it matters.
- Design fits the question.
- Sampling logic, recruitment, and stopping rule are explained.
- Data sources are described.
- Coding process, analytic memos, software/tools, team roles, and iteration are transparent.
- Credibility checks fit the project: member checking, triangulation, negative case analysis, peer debriefing, audit trail, prolonged engagement, or thick description.
- Positionality/reflexivity addresses access, rapport, interpretation, and blind spots.
- Results provide evidence and analysis, not only illustrative quotations.
- Claims are bounded by context and data.

## Mixed Methods

Check:
- Specific design is identified: convergent, explanatory sequential, exploratory sequential, embedded, multiphase, or another justified structure.
- The research question requires both quantitative and qualitative evidence.
- Each strand is methodologically adequate on its own terms.
- Timing, priority, sampling relationship, and integration point are clear.
- Joint displays, merged themes, explained outliers, or connecting cases show integration.
- Contradictions between strands are analyzed, not hidden.
- The mixed inference says something neither strand could support alone.

## Survey Research

Check:
- Survey questions are clear, unbiased, relevant, and not overloaded.
- Open-ended and closed-ended items serve distinct roles and are integrated if used together.
- Gamified or interactive survey design does not distort responses.
- Mode effects are considered: phone, web, app, text, email, in-person, panel.
- Declining response rates, unknown reach, and nonresponse bias are addressed.
- Opt-in panel limits are stated: representativeness depends on the panel, quotas, data quality controls, and research question.
- Administrative or behavioral data are not treated as direct substitutes for beliefs, experiences, motivations, or attitudes.

## Program And Policy Evaluation

Check:
- Needs and objectives are defined.
- Program theory or logic model links inputs, activities, outputs, outcomes, and mechanisms.
- Participant selection/enrollment is clear.
- Impact assessment uses an adequate comparison or causal logic.
- Process evaluation examines implementation, fidelity, dosage, reach, barriers, and provider/participant experience.
- Mixed methods are considered when both impact and implementation matter.
- Outcome timing fits the expected mechanism.
- Cost-effectiveness or cost-benefit is addressed when policy choice depends on resources.
- Ethical implications of program failure, withholding treatment, or reporting negative results are considered.
