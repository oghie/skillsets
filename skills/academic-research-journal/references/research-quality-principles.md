# Research Quality Principles

Use this file to calibrate judgment before applying section or method checklists.

## Realistic Evaluation

- Research often studies narrow problems. Narrowness is acceptable when the contribution is clear and the claims stay narrow.
- Artificial settings can be valid when control matters, but they limit realism and transfer.
- Journal articles omit details. Missing detail is an evaluative finding, not an invitation to guess.
- Journal reputation is weak prior evidence. It never replaces direct evaluation of the article.
- Most methods have flaws. Ask whether the flaw is fatal, mitigated, acknowledged, and proportionate to the contribution.
- Definitions matter. Track conceptual definitions and operational measurement for each key construct.
- Theory matters when it guides questions, constructs, hypotheses, interpretation, or contribution. Decorative theory is weak.
- Replication matters. No single study proves a broad claim.
- Expertise comes from knowing the evidence base and the strengths and weaknesses of major studies, not from repeating broad claims.

## When A Flaw Is More Forgivable

- The ideal method is infeasible, unethical, or unavailable.
- The authors use credible mitigation.
- The limitation is acknowledged with specific implications.
- The study addresses an important, timely, difficult, or under-researched problem.
- The claim is framed cautiously.
- The work contributes pilot evidence, a dataset, a measure, a method, a theory refinement, or policy-relevant descriptive evidence.

## When A Flaw Is Less Forgivable

- A better method was feasible.
- The flaw directly invalidates the main inference.
- The manuscript hides, minimizes, or euphemizes the limitation.
- The conclusion depends on an unsupported assumption.
- The article uses broad causal, population, or policy language beyond the design.
- The paper treats statistical significance as practical or theoretical importance.

## Missing Information Protocol

Use this pattern:

```text
I/I: The manuscript does not provide enough information to judge this criterion.
Needed information: ...
Effect on evaluation: ...
Revision: ...
```

## Score Scale

```text
5 = Strong / publication-ready / methodologically persuasive
4 = Mostly strong, with minor correctable issues
3 = Adequate but limited; publishable only with revision or cautious framing
2 = Weak; major flaws threaten interpretation or contribution
1 = Unacceptable / invalid / not journal-ready
N/A = Not applicable
I/I = Insufficient information to judge
```

## Calibrated Language

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| This proves X causes Y. | The evidence is consistent with X affecting Y, but the design must support causal inference before using causal language. |
| The sample is bad. | The sample fits exploratory insight but does not support broad population generalization. |
| The study is useless. | The study has serious limitations but may still contribute as pilot, descriptive, methodological, or theory-generating evidence. |
| The literature review is weak. | The review summarizes sources but does not synthesize debates, contradictions, methods, or gaps. |
| The finding is significant, so it matters. | The statistical result needs magnitude, uncertainty, practical importance, and robustness context. |

## High-Risk Signals

- Research question absent or hidden.
- Contribution framed only as "fills a gap".
- Theory named but not used.
- Undefined key constructs.
- Measures do not match constructs.
- Sampling does not match generalization.
- Causal wording from non-causal design.
- No limitations section.
- No ethics statement for human-participant research.
- Results do not answer the stated question.
- Discussion introduces major new evidence not prepared earlier.
- Systematic review lacks transparent search and inclusion criteria.
- Meta-analysis ignores heterogeneity or publication bias.
- Mixed methods study merely juxtaposes strands without integration.
- Qualitative results provide quotes without analysis.
