## Communication Principle
Different audiences need different altitude:
- Board/shareholders: business outcome, risk, capital allocation, accountability, decision asks.
- CEO/CFO/COO/CRO: trade-offs, cost, risk, delivery, operating impact.
- Product/business peers: customer outcome, sequencing, dependencies, constraints.
- Engineering teams: context, priorities, decision logic, quality bar.
- Audit/regulators: evidence, controls, ownership, remediation.
- Customers/partners: trust, commitments, impact, next steps.

## Board Reporting
Board report should include:
- current state.
- key changes since last report.
- strategic progress.
- material risks.
- cyber posture and incidents if material.
- investment asks.
- decision deadlines.
- metrics with trend.
- actions and owners.

Do not bury a material risk in appendix.

## Executive Advisory
Frame advice as:
1. Decision needed.
2. Options.
3. Recommendation.
4. Trade-offs.
5. Financial impact.
6. Risk impact.
7. People and execution impact.
8. Validation plan.

## Pushback
Push back when:
- requested scope exceeds capacity.
- security/compliance risk is unacceptable.
- deadline requires hidden quality loss.
- budget assumption is false.
- dependency owner is missing.
- business outcome is unclear.

Use evidence and options, not complaint. State what can be true if the leader chooses speed, cost, risk, or scope.

## Presentation
Good executive presentations:
- start with conclusion.
- show one decision per section.
- quantify where evidence exists.
- call out assumptions.
- include risks and mitigations.
- use visuals for systems, roadmap, scorecard, risk map, and operating model.

Presentation quality checks:
- one major idea per section.
- audience value is explicit.
- the ask is visible before the appendix.
- evidence and assumptions are separated.
- visuals reduce cognitive load instead of decorating slides.
- speaker can explain the chart without reading it.

## Meeting Design
Every leadership meeting needs:
- purpose.
- decision or input needed.
- pre-read.
- owner.
- time box.
- attendees by role.
- output.
- follow-up owner and date.

Cancel recurring meetings that do not create decisions, alignment, learning, or accountability.

## Narrative
Use story ethically:
- current reality.
- tension or risk.
- desired future.
- path and trade-offs.
- role of each stakeholder.
- evidence and next action.

Good leadership narrative does not replace evidence. It makes the evidence actionable for a specific audience.

## Storytelling
Use storytelling when facts alone are not moving behavior.

Structure:
1. Name the human or business context.
2. Show the tension: risk, opportunity, constraint, or customer pain.
3. Connect to evidence.
4. Make the choice clear.
5. State the role each audience member plays.
6. Close with the next decision or behavior.

Avoid:
- exaggerating certainty.
- using fear without a credible action path.
- burying the moral in technical detail.
- making the leader the hero when the organization needs shared ownership.

## Large Group Communication
For town halls, all-hands, crisis updates, or cross-functional forums:
- open with what changed and why it matters.
- speak at the altitude of the whole audience.
- name what is known, unknown, and still being decided.
- repeat decision logic, not only decision outcome.
- leave time for real questions.
- answer hard questions without punishing the questioner.
- follow up in writing with decisions, owners, and dates.

Large groups amplify inconsistency. Do not improvise material policy, headcount, security, incident, or customer commitment statements without verification.

## Critical Incidents
Incidents are often mishandled because leaders over-focus on agenda, defensiveness, or blame.

During a critical incident:
- stabilize people and customer impact first.
- name decision owner and communication owner.
- keep technical, customer, legal, security, and executive channels coordinated.
- distinguish fact, hypothesis, and action.
- record decisions and timestamps.
- communicate uncertainty plainly.
- avoid premature root cause.
- protect psychological safety without lowering accountability.

After the incident:
- run a learning review.
- identify one or two material control improvements.
- assign owners and dates.
- report residual risk.
- update operating process, not only the incident ticket.

## Red Flags
- Slides show progress but no decision needed.
- Technical detail hides business risk.
- Board cyber report is only compliance status.
- Teams hear decisions after rumors spread.
- Pushback happens too late to be useful.
- Story is emotionally persuasive but unsupported by evidence.
- Leader answers beyond verified facts in a regulated or crisis context.
