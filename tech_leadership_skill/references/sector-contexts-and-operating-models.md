## Sector Lens
Before recommending leadership strategy, identify:
- revenue or mission model.
- regulatory exposure.
- safety impact.
- data sensitivity.
- operational continuity requirements.
- customer trust model.
- procurement and vendor dependence.
- technology maturity.
- cybersecurity threat profile.
- audit style.

## Sector Forces

| Sector | Leadership emphasis |
|---|---|
| Banking/financial services | resilience, cyber, identity, fraud, regulatory evidence, audit, change control, vendor risk |
| Insurance | risk models, legacy modernization, data governance, privacy, distribution partners, claims operations |
| Oil, gas, energy | OT/ICS safety, reliability, cyber-physical risk, asset lifecycle, field operations, vendor access |
| Government | procurement, sovereignty, public accountability, compliance, legacy integration, budget cycles |
| Defense/security | classified/sensitive data, supply chain, mission assurance, strict access, resilience, auditability |
| Healthcare | patient safety, privacy, clinical workflow, uptime, medical devices, consent, regulated data |
| Manufacturing | OT, quality systems, production continuity, supply chain, plant-level change control |
| Logistics/supply chain | real-time operations, partner integration, route/network resilience, data sharing, uptime |
| FMCG/retail | omnichannel, demand forecasting, supply chain, customer data, margin discipline |
| Agriculture | field connectivity, IoT, seasonal cycles, commodity exposure, local operations |
| Real estate | asset management, tenant/customer platforms, data fragmentation, vendor systems |
| Media/education/non-profit | budget constraints, content/data rights, accessibility, mission alignment |
| Technology/SaaS | product velocity, platform scale, security trust, customer success, SOC/audit reports |
| Vendor/consulting | utilization, client confidentiality, delivery governance, knowledge reuse, partner ecosystem |

## Geography And Multinational Context
Adjust for:
- local regulation.
- board expectations.
- labor and contracting norms.
- procurement.
- language.
- power distance.
- compliance enforcement.
- data residency.
- cross-border transfer.
- time zone and regional autonomy.

Current legal, regulatory, tax, labor, data residency, and sector-specific requirements require live verification. Say exactly: `This needs verification.`

## Operating Model Choice
Common models:
- Centralized technology: strong standards, risk control, slower local responsiveness.
- Federated technology: local speed, risk of duplication and inconsistent controls.
- Platform model: shared capabilities and guardrails, requires product thinking and service ownership.
- Embedded product engineering: close to business, risk of fragmented architecture.
- Outsourced/vendor-heavy: speed and capacity, risk of dependency and weak internal capability.
- Hybrid: often realistic, but requires clear decision rights.

## Red Flags
- Copying a Big Tech model into a regulated bank without control adjustment.
- Applying startup speed norms in safety-critical or audit-heavy environments.
- Applying government procurement cadence to a fast product organization.
- Ignoring OT security in energy/manufacturing/logistics.
- Treating multinational alignment as translation rather than decision-rights design.
