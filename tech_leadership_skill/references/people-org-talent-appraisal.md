## Leadership Span
Map:
- direct reports and manager span.
- team missions.
- critical systems owned.
- decision rights.
- succession risk.
- manager maturity.
- overloaded roles.

Red flag: a high-performing engineer is made a tech lead without explicit expectations, mentoring, and authority.

## Hiring And Retention
Hiring plan must connect to portfolio and operating model:
- role needed.
- business capability enabled.
- skills required now vs later.
- onboarding burden.
- manager capacity.
- compensation band.
- retention risk if not hired.

Retention is not only compensation. Review autonomy, clarity, manager quality, growth path, workload, incident burden, recognition, fairness, and belief in strategy.

## Manager System
Expected manager behaviors:
- regular 1:1s with purpose.
- direct feedback before appraisal season.
- delegation with context and authority.
- career development.
- conflict handling.
- performance documentation.
- calibration participation.
- escalation without blame dumping.

## Managing Managers
Managing managers is not simply managing more people. Information arrives through another abstraction layer, so the leader must actively inspect team health and manager behavior.

Required practices:
- proactive skip-level meetings.
- manager accountability based on team health and outcomes.
- explicit expectations for managers.
- coaching first-time managers differently from experienced managers.
- hiring managers for values, management philosophy, and team fit.
- reviewing goals, recruiting, postmortems, delivery health, and people issues without taking the manager's job away.

The open-door policy is not enough. People rarely escalate serious issues upward just because the door is open. Leaders must seek signal before attrition, missed projects, or team dysfunction becomes expensive.

## Technical Relevance For Managers
Senior managers do not need to be the deepest engineer, but must remain technical enough to:
- ask informed questions.
- evaluate technical investments.
- analyze engineering/business trade-offs.
- make specific requests.
- avoid becoming a message-passing layer between executives and teams.

Keep relevance by reading code or reviews occasionally, learning unfamiliar areas from engineers, attending postmortems, tracking development/deploy/monitoring practices, maintaining external technical peers, and continuing to learn.

## Performance Appraisal
Evaluate:
- outcomes delivered.
- behavior and collaboration.
- technical judgment.
- operational ownership.
- learning and growth.
- leadership at level.
- risk and quality discipline.

Use evidence, not memory. Appraisal periods are biased by recency, visibility, politics, and manager preference.

Appraisal should not create surprise. If the employee first hears critical feedback in the formal review, the manager system failed.

## Continuous Feedback
Use frequent feedback to make appraisal a synthesis, not a trial.

Minimum loop:
- role expectation and level expectation.
- regular 1:1 signal gathering.
- feedback tied to observed behavior and impact.
- development action with date.
- evidence log.
- mid-cycle calibration for risk cases.
- formal review that summarizes known evidence.

Manager questions:
- Did I give feedback close enough to the event?
- Did I separate fact, interpretation, and expectation?
- Did I check context before judgment?
- Did I document both impact and growth?
- Did the person have a fair chance to respond and improve?

## Appraisal Dimensions
Use dimensions that reflect engineering leadership work, not only visible feature output:

| Dimension | What to inspect |
|---|---|
| Attitude and ownership | reliability, initiative, pressure handling, mission alignment |
| Collaboration and communication | listening, customer service, negotiation, meeting behavior, written clarity |
| Creative thinking | problem solving, innovation, improvement, change adoption |
| Ethics and judgment | fairness, integrity, inclusion, professionalism, sustainability |
| Job knowledge and expertise | technical knowledge, research, mentoring, analytical skill, technology support |
| Leadership | coaching, delegation, conflict handling, hiring, feedback, decision making |
| Planning and administration | goals, schedules, cost control, priority setting, deadlines |
| Quality and quantity | accuracy, detail discipline, productivity, remote work effectiveness |
| Self-development | learning, upskilling, career planning, response to coaching |

Write appraisal language as evidence-based observations:

```text
Observed behavior: [specific action].
Impact: [business/customer/team/risk result].
Expectation at level: [standard].
Next development action: [behavior, support, date].
```

Avoid generic praise or criticism. Do not copy phrasebooks mechanically; generate wording from evidence.

## 360 Calibration
Collect signals from:
- manager.
- peers.
- direct reports.
- product/business partners.
- security/operations/audit partners when relevant.
- self-review.

Calibration questions:
1. Is the rating evidence-based?
2. Are similar contributions rated similarly?
3. Did invisible operational, security, or mentoring work get valued?
4. Did high-visibility heroics mask preventable chaos?
5. Are bias and proximity effects controlled?

Bias checks:
- recency.
- similarity affinity.
- halo from one major project.
- penalty for invisible maintenance or mentoring.
- cultural communication style mismatch.
- manager conflict of interest.
- visibility gap for remote or platform work.

## Incentives
Incentives shape behavior. Check whether current incentives reward:
- shipping without operability.
- hiding security risk.
- local optimization over company outcome.
- hero culture over sustainable systems.
- starting new projects over finishing or retiring work.
- headcount growth over productivity.

## Organization Design
Use Conway-aware mapping:
- Teams should own coherent domains or platforms.
- Interfaces between teams should match architecture boundaries.
- Shared services need clear service model, prioritization, and SLO.
- Security and platform teams should provide guardrails and enablement, not only approval queues.

## Red Flags
- Performance review surprises employees.
- Critical systems depend on one person.
- Manager span is too wide for coaching.
- Career ladder rewards only feature delivery.
- Appraisal ignores security, reliability, mentoring, and operational ownership.
- Reorg is used to avoid hard strategy or personnel decisions.
